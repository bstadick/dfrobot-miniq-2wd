522 
523 /*
524 * The encoders on the wheels are gear tooth counters. They trigger an external
525 * interrupt every time a gear tooth passes by. There are a total of 12 teeth
526 * on each wheel. Each wheel is about 4cm in diameter making the resolution of
527 * each tick on the counter to be about 1.05cm. The count is handled in a separate
528 * ISR that catches every tick and uses the current direction of the motor
529 * control to determine if the count should be incremented or decremented.
530 * <p>
531 * Since the tick count will change as the wheel spins, it is important to check
532 * the count often to make sure you have moved the distance you would like and correct
533 * accordingly. You can either either poll the count number and check for changes,
534 * or you can add your own code to the ISR. It is important that you make ISRs
535 * fast, as they will interrupt any other code running and will not relinquish control
536 * until done. This means long running operations in an ISR may cause other ISRs
537 * to not run when needed (and thus miss ticks on the other wheel).
538 * <p>
539 */

766 //**************************** Obstacle Detection ****************************
767 
768 /*
769 * The IR obstacle detection above tends not to be the most robust method for
770 * detecting objects in your path. Instead it is recommended that you wire up a
771 * pair of micro switches, one on the front and one on the back. This will give a
772 * more robust way of telling if a wall is in your way.
773 * <p>
774 * The wiring is as described. You wire the normally open (NO) node of the micro
775 * switch to the cathode of the IR LED (negative terminal). You can wire switch one
776 * to the right IR LED and switch two to the left IR LED. You do not need to remove
777 * the LEDs for this to work. The common node of each micro switch is wired to ground
778 * through a 1Kohm or 10Kohm pull down resistor. Both can be wired to the same resistor.
779 * The pull down resistor is then wired to the ground on the robot. There are several
780 * options for doing this. The easiest is to connect the resistor to a single standard
781 * 0.1" pitch receptacle, and then plug the receptacle onto the bottom-left pin of
782 * the ICSP header (the pin closest to the batteries and wheel).
783 * <p>
784 * This setup will allow you to tell when you hit a wall or other object. Both IR
785 * LED pins will be pulled low when you hit either switch one or switch two. This
786 * means you can't detect direction, except by inferring it through your current
787 * direction of travel. In theory, you could add more micro switches and determine
788 * which one was pressed based on your current direction of travel.
789 * <p>
790 * To use the switches, you will need to set the IR LED pins to inputs and detect
791 * when they are false. To regain use of your IR LEDs, simple change the pins to
792 * outputs and unplug the pull-down resistor from the ICSP ground pin.
793 * <p>
794 */
795

1009 /*
1010 * No wireless communication functions are present in this library. The DFRobot
1011 * MiniQ 2WD robot platform uses standard Xbee compatible radios to communicate
1012 * wirelessly. The robot use the Rx/Tx USART module of the microcontroller to send
1013 * and receive data. For those more familiar with the Arduino libraries, this
1014 * functionality is obtained through the Serial library (most commonly associated
1015 * with the USB port).
1016 * <p>
1017 * The DFRobot MiniQ 2WD has two points where the USART is found: in the USART header
1018 * at the front of the robot and at the wireless headers in the back. Both of these
1019 * ports use the same Rx/Tx pins (as this chip has only one USART module), therefore
1020 * both wired and wireless serial communication cannot be used at the same time.
1021 * <p>
1022 * Compatible radios include any Xbee based radio (such as those made by Digi International).
1023 * These radios are usually 3.3V (instead of 5V), come in several different communication
1024 * protocols (such as Bluetooth, ZigBee, DigiMesh, IEEE 802.15.4, and IEEE 802.11 b/g/n (aka Wi-Fi)),
1025 * and have a similar/compatible pin layout (so they're interchangeable).
1026 * You must be careful thought to make sure all the radios you are networking are
1027 * using the same protocol. While they are pin compatible, they can only communicate
1028 * to other radios using the same protocol.
1029 * <p>
1030 * The wireless port on the DFRobot MiniQ 2WD should be able to accept any Xbee
1031 * based radio, as it converts voltages to 3.3V levels and is pin compatible with
1032 * most common Xbee based radios.
1033 * <p>
1034 * For more information and to make sure you are using compatible radios, see
1035 * {@link https://www.sparkfun.com/pages/xbee_guide}
1036 * <p>
1037 */

1039 //**************************** On-board LEDs & Other ****************************
1040 
1041 /*
1042 * The two black sensors on the front are light intensity sensors. They are wired
1043 * together in a voltage divider through the W1 potentiometer. This configuration
1044 * allows for the determining the direction of a light source. The higher the light
1045 * intensity to the right, the lower the voltage ratio and vice-versa. This value is
1046 * tunable using the W1 potentiometer to correct for any bias or create bias to
